\begin{abstract}
\noindent We reassess machine-learning directional predictability on
a single canonical large-cap U.S.\ equity (AAPL, 2020-2024) under
leakage-safe nested Purged K-Fold cross-validation and evaluate
whether remaining predictive signals carry economic value once
realistic trading frictions are imposed. The empirical evidence is
deliberately single-asset; a panel-wide replication across the
other 24 tickers in our universe is gated on multi-day compute and
is left for a follow-up. On a single canonical
asset (AAPL, 2020-2024), a published-style replication that selects
features (Boruta) and tunes hyperparameters (Optuna) on the full
sample reproduces a 73\,\% one-day directional accuracy of the type
commonly reported in the applied literature. The same model,
retrained inside a leakage-safe nested protocol that quarantines
selection and tuning inside the outer training slice, achieves
51\,\% with a Politis-Romano stationary block-bootstrap 95\,\%
confidence interval of $[0.486, 0.541]$ and a $z$-statistic of
$-17$ against the published-style 0.730. At fifteen days the
leakage-safe estimate is statistically tied with chance once the
serial correlation in the correctness series (lag-1 autocorrelation
of approximately $0.69$) is honestly accounted for via the block
bootstrap. Pairwise Diebold-Mariano tests with
Harvey-Leybourne-Newbold corrections rank Random Forest first at
horizons of one and five days and XGBoost at fifteen, but in
absolute terms every model is statistically indistinguishable from
a coin flip at horizons above one day. We translate the
leakage-safe predictions into a long/short sign-following strategy
with realistic transaction costs at $10$ basis points per round
trip; no model-horizon combination exceeds passive buy-and-hold over
the same out-of-fold window. SHAP analysis identifies the Relative
Strength Index as the dominant predictor across architectures.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

This paper reassesses machine-learning directional predictability
on a single canonical large-cap U.S.\ equity (AAPL, 2020-2024)
under leakage-safe validation and evaluates whether remaining
predictive signals carry economic value once realistic trading
frictions are imposed. We focus on AAPL because it is the
single most-studied stock in the applied directional-forecasting
literature, with multiple published claims of accuracy above
70~percent at the daily horizon; this lets us anchor the
reassessment to a target the reader already has a prior for. Reported directional accuracies in
the applied literature commonly fall between 70 and 95 percent for
tree-based and deep models on individual equities, including
high-volume names such as AAPL. We examine whether those headline
numbers survive a validation protocol that quarantines every choice
influenced by the test labels. \\

\noindent Three observations frame the analysis. First, surveys of
the field document that feature engineering and hyperparameter
tuning are routinely performed before, not inside, the validation
split, and many studies use plain K-fold on serially dependent
return series \citep{sezer2020financial,jiang2021applications,htun2023survey}.
Second, the broader machine-learning-for-science literature
identifies data leakage as the most frequent driver of the
reproducibility crisis, with Kapoor and Narayanan cataloguing
seventeen affected fields and showing that corrected pipelines
often eliminate the apparent advantage of complex models over
simpler baselines \citep{kapoor2023leakage}. Third, even when
purged or combinatorial purged cross-validation is adopted
\citep{lopez2018advances,bailey2017dangers}, tuning is typically
performed on the same asset that is later evaluated, leaving an
asset-specific channel through which information from the holdout
still leaks. \\

\noindent We construct a nested Purged K-Fold protocol that places
Boruta feature selection and Optuna hyperparameter search strictly
inside the outer training slice. Boruta is run only on
training-fold data, the Optuna study optimises an inner-fold purged
score, and the held-out outer fold is touched once. Inference on
the resulting correctness series uses the Politis-Romano stationary
bootstrap with the Politis-White block-length rule
\citep{politis1994stationary,politis2004automatic,patton2009correction},
together with Diebold-Mariano tests under Harvey-Leybourne-Newbold
small-sample corrections \citep{diebold1995comparing,harvey1997testing}
and Newey-West HAC standard errors \citep{newey1987simple}. The lag-1
autocorrelation of the AAPL correctness series rises from
approximately $-0.05$ at $h=1$ to $+0.69$ at $h=15$, which renders
the iid binomial confidence interval misleading at longer horizons.
\\

\noindent The empirical results are stark. A published-style
replication that selects features and tunes hyperparameters on the
full sample reproduces a 73~percent directional accuracy on AAPL at
$h=1$. The same model, retrained inside the leakage-safe nested
protocol on the same data and the same outer test fold, achieves
51~percent, with a stationary block-bootstrap 95~percent confidence
interval of $[0.486, 0.541]$. At $h=15$ the leakage-safe estimate
is statistically tied with chance once block-bootstrap and
Diebold-Mariano machinery are applied. The published-style and
leakage-safe accuracy series differ at $z$ below $-17$. \\

\noindent Statistical predictability above chance is necessary but
not sufficient for economic value, a distinction repeatedly
emphasised in the recent financial-ML literature
\citep{avramov2023machine,kelly2023financial} and reinforced by
the multiple-testing concerns surveyed by \citet{harvey2016cross}.
We translate the leakage-safe predictions into long/short
sign-following trading rules with realistic per-trade transaction
costs at $10$ basis points per round trip, a level consistent with
the cost-aware portfolio-construction framework of
\citet{demiguel2020transaction}, and evaluate the resulting net-of
cost annualised returns against passive buy-and-hold on AAPL. None
of the rules exceed the buy-and-hold benchmark over the same
out-of-fold window.
The contribution of the paper is therefore methodological and
empirical at once: a transparent, single-asset reassessment that
demonstrates how much of the published machine-learning advantage
on a canonical stock disappears when validation is honest, and a
frank report that no examined strategy beats a passive benchmark
net of trading costs. \\

\noindent The remainder of the paper is organised as follows.
Section~\ref{sec:methodology} presents the methodology;
Section~\ref{sec:data} describes the data and the feature
construction pipeline; Section~\ref{sec:results} reports a
replication of the published-style analysis under standard Purged
K-Fold cross-validation; Section~\ref{sec:addendum} reports the
leakage-safe nested-CV reassessment together with the block-bootstrap
inference and the economic backtest; Section~\ref{sec:conclusion}
concludes.



\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

Our framework has three components: (i)~construction of predictive features from lagged price data, (ii)~definition of a directional target variable using a noise-reduced price series, and (iii)~out-of-sample evaluation via Purged K-Fold cross-validation, which eliminates the information leakage inherent in standard random-split schemes. \\

\noindent Let \(P_t\) denote the closing price at date \(t\), for \(t=1,\dots,T\), and define log-returns as
\begin{equation}
r_t = \ln\left(\frac{P_t}{P_{t-1}}\right).
\end{equation}

\noindent The objective is to predict the direction of the cumulative return over a horizon \(h \in \{1,2,5,10,15\}\) trading days. The problem is thus a binary classification task: estimate $\mathbb{P}(y_t=1\mid X_t)$, where $y_t$ indicates an upward move and $X_t$ is the information set available at~$t$. Directional forecasting is preferred to point prediction because it maps directly to market-timing decisions and is more robust to extreme observations \citep{fischer2018deep,gu2020empirical}.

\subsection{Feature construction and preprocessing}

The explanatory variables are constructed from the historical price series and consist of technical indicators designed to capture trend persistence, momentum effects, and potential reversal patterns. Let
$X_t = (x_{1t},x_{2t},\dots,x_{pt})$
denote the vector of predictors available at time \(t\). \\

\noindent Among the predictors considered, trend indicators are represented by moving averages:
\begin{equation}
MA_t^{(k)} = \frac{1}{k}\sum_{i=0}^{k-1} P_{t-i},
\end{equation}
where \(k\) denotes the window length. Momentum indicators are defined as
\begin{equation}
MOM_t^{(k)} = P_t - P_{t-k}.
\end{equation}
We also consider oscillators such as the Relative Strength Index (RSI), which is based on the ratio between recent average gains and losses:
\begin{equation}
RSI_t = 100 - \frac{100}{1 + RS_t},
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
RS_t = \frac{\text{Average Gain}_t}{\text{Average Loss}_t}.
\end{equation}

\noindent These variables are nonlinear transformations of lagged price information designed to approximate the unknown mapping from observable market dynamics to future returns. Their empirical relevance is supported by \citet{lo2000foundations}, who document statistically significant predictive content in technical patterns, and by \citet{gu2020empirical}, who show that technical features combined with nonlinear models yield measurable gains in forecast accuracy on cross-sectional asset-pricing tasks. Tree-based ensembles are particularly well suited to this feature class because they can exploit threshold effects and interactions without parametric restrictions. \\

\noindent \textbf{Label smoothing.} Financial closing prices contain substantial high-frequency noise from microstructure effects, transient liquidity shocks, and bid--ask bounce. Because our target variable is the \emph{sign} of the forward price change, even a small perturbation near zero can flip a label, injecting classification noise that destabilizes both training and evaluation. \citet{lopez2018advances} identifies noisy labels as a primary source of instability in financial machine learning. \\

\noindent To mitigate this, we apply a smoothing operator $S(\cdot)$ to the closing price series \emph{used exclusively for label construction}, all technical indicators are computed on raw, unsmoothed OHLCV data and therefore retain full sensitivity to market conditions. Formally, let
\begin{equation}
\tilde{P}_t = S(P_t)
\end{equation}
denote the denoised price. We implement $S(\cdot)$ as a Discrete Wavelet Transform (DWT) filter using a Daubechies-4 mother wavelet \citep{donoho1994ideal}. The procedure decomposes $P_t$ into approximation and detail coefficients at each resolution level, applies soft thresholding to the detail coefficients to attenuate noise, and reconstructs the denoised series. Compared with exponential smoothing, which \citet{basak2019predicting} apply to prices \emph{before} computing indicators, thereby double-smoothing, wavelet denoising offers two advantages: (i)~it preserves sharp trend transitions (important for capturing regime changes around events such as the March~2020 crash), and (ii)~it introduces less systematic lag because the filter adapts locally in both time and frequency. In our data, wavelet denoising achieves approximately 67.5\% noise reduction versus 78.9\% for exponential smoothing; the lower figure reflects the preservation of economically relevant high-frequency content rather than inferior denoising. \\

\noindent The choice of the Daubechies-4 wavelet and the automatic decomposition-level selection (set by PyWavelets to $\lfloor \log_2(T) \rfloor$) follow standard practice in signal processing \citep{donoho1994ideal}. No alternative wavelet families were tested, and the decomposition level was not tuned to prediction accuracy. This design decision limits the risk of in-sample over-optimization of the denoising step, though it leaves open the possibility that a different filter could yield marginally better or worse results, a limitation we acknowledge. \\

\noindent It is important to emphasize that smoothing operates solely on the dependent variable (label construction), not on the feature space. This asymmetric treatment avoids the double-smoothing problem noted above and ensures that the predictors retain the full information content of the raw market data.

\noindent \textbf{Wavelet smoothing is non-causal.} The DWT
filter described above is a two-sided filter: at any date~$t$
it weights observations at $t', t' > t$, when reconstructing the
denoised series. Wavelet-smoothed labels therefore embed
forward-looking information that would not be available to a
real-time forecaster. We retain the wavelet-smoothed protocol
in this section because it describes the originally-implemented
pipeline whose accuracy figures are reported in
Section~\ref{sec:results}, but the leakage-safe reassessment in
Section~\ref{sec:addendum} replaces it with raw realised returns
$y_t = \mathbf{1}\{P_{t+h} > P_t\}$, a strictly causal label,
and treats the wavelet variant as a robustness comparison only.
The interaction between the leakage-safe protocol and label
smoothing is analysed in
\texttt{docs/audit/03\_label\_leakage\_report.md} of the
companion repository.

\subsection{Target variable, model estimation, and feature selection}

The target variable is the direction of the forward price change over horizon~$h$:
\begin{equation}
y_t =
\begin{cases}
1 & \text{if } \tilde{P}_{t+h} > \tilde{P}_t, \\
0 & \text{otherwise,}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
where $\tilde{P}_t$ is the wavelet-denoised closing price. The estimation objective is the conditional probability $\mathbb{P}(y_t = 1 \mid X_t)$, with $X_t$ restricted to information available at date~$t$. \\

\noindent With 28~candidate predictors (14~indicators plus their first differences) and roughly 1{,}250~observations per stock, overfitting is a material concern. We therefore apply a two-stage feature selection procedure, correlation filtering followed by the Boruta algorithm \citep{kursa2010feature}, described in detail in Section~\ref{sec:feat_sel}. The surviving 13~features are used in all subsequent estimation. \\

\noindent Prediction models are tree-based ensembles of the general form
\begin{equation}
f(X) = \sum_{m=1}^{M} \gamma_m \, h_m(X),
\end{equation}
where $h_m(\cdot)$ are decision trees and $\gamma_m$ their weights. We consider five variants: Random Forest \citep{breiman2001random}, Gradient Boosting \citep{friedman2001greedy}, XGBoost \citep{chen2016xgboost}, LightGBM \citep{ke2017lightgbm}, and CatBoost \citep{prokhorenkova2018catboost}. These models share the ability to capture nonlinear threshold effects and predictor interactions without parametric assumptions, but differ in their regularization strategies, splitting heuristics, and handling of class imbalance, differences that may matter in noisy financial data.

\subsection{Cross-validation, overlapping labels, and information leakage}

Standard K-fold cross-validation assumes i.i.d.\ observations. In financial time series, this assumption fails for two reasons: serial dependence in features and, more critically, the fact that labels constructed over a forward horizon~$h$ depend on an \emph{interval} of future prices rather than a single date. When the label intervals of a training observation and a test observation overlap, the two share common future realizations and the train--test split is no longer informationally clean. \\

\noindent Let the sample be partitioned into $K$ folds, $D = \bigcup_{k=1}^{K} D_k$. To formalize the leakage mechanism, consider for simplicity the unsmoothed label
\begin{equation}
y_t = \mathbf{1}\{P_{t+h} > P_t\}.
\end{equation}
\noindent More generally, the same argument applies when labels are defined using the smoothed series \(\tilde{P}_t\). The key point is that \(y_t\) depends on information extending beyond time \(t\). Let
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{I}_t^{(h)} = \sigma(P_t,P_{t+1},\dots,P_{t+h})
\end{equation}
\noindent denote the information set used to construct the label at time \(t\). Then \(y_t\) is measurable with respect to \(\mathcal{I}_t^{(h)}\). \\

\noindent Now consider two observations indexed by \(t\) and \(s\), with \(t < s\). Their labels depend respectively on
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{I}_t^{(h)} = \sigma(P_t,\dots,P_{t+h}),
\qquad
\mathcal{I}_s^{(h)} = \sigma(P_s,\dots,P_{s+h}).
\end{equation}
If \(s \leq t+h\), then the intervals \([t,t+h]\) and \([s,s+h]\) overlap, and therefore
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{I}_t^{(h)} \cap \mathcal{I}_s^{(h)} \neq \emptyset.
\end{equation}
This implies that \(y_t\) and \(y_s\) are constructed using some common future realizations of the price process. Hence, they are mechanically dependent even when the underlying one-period returns exhibit weak dependence. \\

\noindent This overlap can be made explicit by defining labels from cumulative returns:
\begin{equation}
y_t = \mathbf{1}\{R_{t,t+h}>0\},
\qquad
R_{t,t+h} = \sum_{j=1}^{h} r_{t+j}.
\end{equation}
Similarly,
\begin{equation}
y_s = \mathbf{1}\{R_{s,s+h}>0\},
\qquad
R_{s,s+h} = \sum_{j=1}^{h} r_{s+j}.
\end{equation}
If \(s=t+1\), then
\begin{equation}
R_{t,t+h} = r_{t+1}+r_{t+2}+\cdots+r_{t+h},
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}
R_{t+1,t+1+h} = r_{t+2}+r_{t+3}+\cdots+r_{t+h+1}.
\end{equation}
These two cumulative returns share the common terms
\begin{equation}
r_{t+2},r_{t+3},\dots,r_{t+h}.
\end{equation}
Therefore, even under the simplifying assumption that one-period returns are serially uncorrelated with constant variance \(\sigma^2\), we obtain
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Cov}(R_{t,t+h},R_{t+1,t+1+h}) = \sum_{j=2}^{h}\mathrm{Var}(r_{t+j}) = (h-1)\sigma^2.
\end{equation}
More generally, for any two starting dates \(t\) and \(s\),
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Cov}(R_{t,t+h},R_{s,s+h})
=
\#\big([t+1,t+h]\cap[s+1,s+h]\big)\sigma^2,
\end{equation}
under independent and homoskedastic returns, where \(\#(\cdot)\) denotes the number of overlapping periods. Hence, whenever \(|t-s|<h\), the covariance is strictly positive. \\

\noindent This result provides the formal justification for the information leakage problem. If one observation belongs to the training set and another overlapping observation belongs to the test set, the model is effectively trained and evaluated on labels that share common future realizations. In that case, the training and testing samples are not informationally separated. Standard random K-fold cross-validation therefore violates the logic of genuine out-of-sample evaluation. This problem is emphasized in \citet{lopez2018advances}, who argues that in financial machine learning the relevant unit is not the timestamp itself but the full label interval associated with that observation. \\

\noindent The issue becomes even stronger when predictors are themselves constructed from rolling windows. For example, if one feature is a moving average
\begin{equation}
MA_t^{(k)} = \frac{1}{k}\sum_{i=0}^{k-1}P_{t-i},
\end{equation}
\noindent then neighboring observations may share both highly similar regressors and partially identical future outcomes. This double overlap can produce overly optimistic performance measures if the train-test split is not designed carefully. \\

\noindent To address this problem, we adopt the Purged K-Fold cross-validation procedure proposed by \citet{lopez2018advances}. Let \(Test = D_j\) denote the test fold. The training sample is defined as
\begin{equation}
Train = D \setminus (Test \cup Embargo),
\end{equation}
\noindent where the embargo period is a buffer zone around the test observations. The purging rule removes from the training set every observation whose label interval overlaps with the test set interval. Formally, if a test observation indexed by \(u\) has label interval
\begin{equation}
I_u = [u,u+h],
\end{equation}
then any training observation \(t\) such that
\begin{equation}
I_t \cap I_u \neq \emptyset
\end{equation}
must be excluded. Equivalently, observation \(t\) is purged whenever
\begin{equation}
[t,t+h]\cap[u,u+h]\neq\emptyset.
\end{equation}
This condition is equivalent to
\begin{equation}
t \leq u+h
\qquad \text{and} \qquad
u \leq t+h.
\end{equation}

\noindent An embargo buffer around the test fold provides additional protection against residual dependence from serial correlation in predictors and rolling-window features. This approach is consistent with the time-series evaluation literature \citep{bergmeir2012use,bergmeir2018note,cerqueira2020evaluating} and with broader concerns about information separation in empirical asset pricing \citep{gu2020empirical}. In our implementation we set $K=5$ with a 1\% embargo, following \citet{lopez2018advances}.

\subsection{Model interpretability}

Predictive accuracy alone does not establish economic relevance. We therefore supplement aggregate performance metrics with SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) decompositions \citep{lundberg2017unified}, which attribute each prediction additively to the contributing features. For tree-based methods, SHAP values can be computed exactly in polynomial time via the TreeSHAP algorithm of \citet{lundberg2020local}. Globally, the mean absolute SHAP value of predictor~$j$ serves as an importance measure comparable across models. Locally, SHAP dependence plots reveal the functional form linking a feature's raw value to its marginal contribution, enabling us to assess, for instance, whether the RSI enters the model as a momentum-continuation signal (monotonically increasing) or as a mean-reversion signal (non-monotonic). This distinction has direct economic content and cannot be inferred from a single importance ranking.

\section{Data}
\label{sec:data}

\subsection{Investment universe and sample period}

Our empirical analysis is based on daily closing prices for 25~U.S.-listed equities drawn from five broad economic sectors: Technology (AAPL, MSFT, GOOGL, NVDA, META), Automotive (TSLA, F, GM, TM, HMC), Consumer (NKE, SBUX, MCD, DIS, NFLX), Financial (JPM, BAC, GS, MS, C), and Healthcare (JNJ, UNH, PFE, ABBV, TMO). The sample runs from January~1,~2020 through December~31,~2024, yielding approximately 1{,}257~trading days per stock. All data are sourced from Yahoo Finance via the \texttt{yfinance} Python API. \\

\noindent This five-year window is selected because it spans a sequence of structurally distinct market regimes: the COVID-19 crash and subsequent recovery (March-August~2020), the speculative retail-trading episode of 2021, the monetary-tightening cycle and equity selloff of 2022, and the technology-led rally driven by the emergence of large language models in 2023--2024. This heterogeneity is desirable because it forces predictive models to generalize across different volatility and trend conditions rather than exploit a single dominant regime. \\

\noindent A preliminary data-quality assessment confirms that all 25~stocks have zero missing observations for the OHLCV fields, so neither imputation nor interpolation is required. The average daily return across the panel is positive but small (consistent with the long-run equity premium), while annualized volatility ranges from roughly 19\% (JNJ, Healthcare) to over 65\% (TSLA, Automotive). Tesla exhibits the most extreme tail behavior, with 44~daily moves exceeding $\pm 10\%$ during the sample period. These distributional properties, fat tails, positive skewness, and heterogeneous volatility, are characteristic of equity return data and motivate the use of nonparametric models that impose no distributional assumptions.

\subsection{Feature construction}

From the raw OHLCV series we compute 14~technical indicators organized into four categories. The \textit{momentum} group comprises the Relative Strength Index (RSI), Stochastic Oscillator~(\%K), Williams~\%R, the MACD histogram, the Price Rate of Change (PROC), and the Commodity Channel Index (CCI). The \textit{volatility} group includes Bollinger Band Width, the Bollinger \%B indicator, the Average True Range (ATR), and 20-day historical volatility. The \textit{volume} group consists of On-Balance Volume (OBV), the Money Flow Index (MFI), and the Accumulation/Distribution Line. Finally, the \textit{trend} category is represented by the Average Directional Index (ADX). The first six indicators replicate the feature set of \citet{basak2019predicting}; the remaining eight extend coverage to volatility, trend strength, and volume-weighted dimensions that are absent from the original study. \\

\noindent Each of the 14~base indicators is augmented by its one-day first difference ($\Delta x_{jt} = x_{jt} - x_{j,t-1}$), yielding 28~candidate features. The change features capture the \emph{dynamics} of each indicator, whether momentum is accelerating, volatility is expanding, or volume pressure is shifting, information that a single cross-sectional snapshot cannot convey. \\

\noindent An important methodological choice concerns the interaction between indicator computation and label smoothing. \citet{basak2019predicting} apply exponential smoothing to the raw price series \emph{before} computing technical indicators, which introduces double-smoothing because most indicators already embed their own internal averaging (e.g., the RSI uses an exponential moving average of gains and losses). In our pipeline, indicators are computed on unsmoothed OHLCV data so that they retain full sensitivity to market conditions. Smoothing is applied only to the closing price series used for label construction, via Discrete Wavelet Transform denoising with a Daubechies-4 mother wavelet \citep{donoho1994ideal}. This wavelet filter achieves approximately 67.5\% noise reduction while preserving sharp transitions better than exponential smoothing (78.9\% noise reduction), which tends to introduce excessive lag (Figure~\ref{fig:smoothing}).

\subsection{Feature selection}
\label{sec:feat_sel}

With 28~candidate predictors and approximately 1{,}250~observations per stock, a disciplined selection procedure is necessary to control estimation variance and remove redundant inputs. We adopt a two-stage pipeline. \\

\noindent \textit{Stage~1: Correlation filter.} We compute the pairwise Pearson correlation matrix of all 28~features and drop one member from every pair whose absolute correlation exceeds~0.9. This threshold eliminates the near-perfect collinearity among the momentum oscillators noted by \citet{basak2019predicting}: the Stochastic Oscillator, Williams~\%R, and CCI/Bollinger~\%B pair all exceed $|\rho| > 0.92$. After this step the candidate set is reduced to approximately 20~features. \\

\noindent \textit{Stage~2: Boruta algorithm.} The surviving features are submitted to the Boruta wrapper method \citep{kursa2010feature}, which creates randomized ``shadow'' copies of each real predictor, trains a Random Forest, and uses a statistical test to determine whether each real feature is significantly more important than the best shadow feature. Features that pass this test are classified as \emph{confirmed}; those that do not are \emph{rejected}. The final selected set contains 13~features, comprising a mix of base indicators and their change counterparts, which are used in all subsequent model estimation. \\

\noindent Figure~\ref{fig:feature_corr} displays the pairwise correlation structure of the 14~base indicators, Figure~\ref{fig:boruta} reports the Boruta importance ranking with confirmed and rejected features clearly demarcated, and Figure~\ref{fig:target_corr} shows the absolute Pearson correlation between each candidate feature and the binary target.

\subsection{Label construction and class balance}

The binary target variable is defined as $y_t = \mathbf{1}\{\tilde{P}_{t+h} > \tilde{P}_t\}$, where $\tilde{P}_t$ denotes the wavelet-denoised closing price and $h \in \{1,2,5,10,15\}$~trading days is the forecasting horizon. Five horizons are considered to assess whether predictability varies with the investment holding period. \\

\noindent Across all horizons the label distribution exhibits a moderate class imbalance of roughly 61\% positive (UP) versus 39\% negative (DOWN), reflecting the structural upward bias of U.S.\ equities during the sample period. This imbalance is addressed in model training via sample weighting proportional to the inverse class frequency, which prevents the classifiers from converging to a trivial majority-class rule.

\section{Results: Published-Style Replication under Standard Purged K-Fold}
\label{sec:results}

This section reports a published-style replication of the
empirical pipeline under \emph{standard} Purged K-Fold cross-
validation, in which feature selection (Boruta) and hyperparameter
tuning (Optuna) are performed once on the full sample before the
outer fold split, as is common in the applied literature. We
report the standard analysis here for two reasons: it documents
the level at which the published-style pipeline arrives, and it
provides the foil against which Section~\ref{sec:addendum}
quantifies the leakage-induced inflation. The headline accuracy
figures reported in this section are superseded by the
nested-CV reassessment of Section~\ref{sec:addendum}, which
quarantines selection and tuning inside the outer training slice
and applies block-bootstrap inference to the resulting
out-of-fold correctness series. Readers primarily interested in
the leakage-safe answer can skip directly to
Section~\ref{sec:addendum}; readers interested in the magnitude
of the leakage-induced inflation should read both sections in
order. \\

\noindent The published-style replication itself is organised
in five parts. We first quantify the accuracy inflation induced
by standard cross-validation relative to Purged K-Fold
(Section~\ref{sec:leakage}). We then compare baseline and
tree-based model performance (Section~\ref{sec:baseline}),
assess the impact of hyperparameter tuning
(Section~\ref{sec:tuning}), examine how accuracy varies across
forecasting horizons and economic sectors
(Sections~\ref{sec:window}, \ref{sec:sector}), and conclude
with model interpretability through SHAP analysis
(Section~\ref{sec:shap}).

\noindent Accuracy declines monotonically from roughly 70--73\% at the one-day horizon to approximately 49--53\% at 15~days. \noindent One-way ANOVA tests confirm that the window effect is highly significant ($p < 0.0001$) for every model, confirming that forecasting horizon is a first-order determinant of predictive performance. From a practical standpoint, these results imply that tree-based directional models are best suited to short-horizon market timing (one to two days) and offer little value for medium-term tactical allocation.

\noindent Several findings merit discussion. First, the Relative Strength Index dominates all five models, contributing between roughly 25\% (LightGBM) and 48\% (CatBoost) of total mean absolute SHAP importance. Economically, the RSI summarizes recent directional momentum over a 14-day lookback. Its dominance at the one-day horizon aligns with the short-term return continuation documented by \citet{jegadeesh1993returns} and \citet{lo2000foundations}: stocks that have risen recently tend to continue rising over the next few days, a pattern that tree-based models can exploit via simple threshold rules on the RSI. \\

\noindent Second, the beeswarm plot (Figure~\ref{fig:shap_bee})
and the per-feature dependence plots (Figure~\ref{fig:shap_dep})
reveal a \emph{monotonically increasing} RSI--SHAP profile: high
RSI values push predictions toward UP, low values toward DOWN.
This functional form is the signature of a momentum-continuation
strategy, not a mean-reversion strategy (which would produce a
non-monotonic or decreasing profile at extreme RSI levels). The absence of a reversal effect at RSI values above~70 suggests that, at the daily horizon, overbought conditions do not yet trigger contrarian pressure, consistent with the well-known finding that momentum profits peak at short horizons before reversing at intermediate ones \citep{jegadeesh1993returns}. \\

\noindent Third, the cross-model comparison (Figure~\ref{fig:shap_cross}) reveals an architectural effect: LightGBM distributes importance more evenly, while CatBoost and Gradient Boosting concentrate heavily on RSI. This difference likely reflects the sequential nature of boosting with deeper trees, the algorithm fits the dominant signal first and allocates residual capacity to secondary features, whereas Random Forest, by averaging many independently grown trees, inherently diversifies across predictors. For practitioners, this implies that ensemble-diversity techniques (e.g., stacking models with different importance profiles) may extract complementary information. \\

\noindent The secondary role of volatility features (ATR, historical volatility) is consistent with the observation that realized volatility carries marginal information about the \emph{direction}, though not the magnitude, of future returns. The near-zero SHAP contribution of volume-based features (OBV, AD~Line) at the daily horizon suggests that volume dynamics are largely priced into the momentum indicators and add little incremental signal.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We reassess the directional predictability of stock returns under
a leakage-safe nested Purged K-Fold protocol on AAPL over the
2020-2024 sample, accompanied by stationary block-bootstrap
inference and an economic backtest after realistic costs. Three
findings emerge. \\

\noindent \textit{Validation, more than model choice, drives
reported accuracy.} The published-style replication of
Section~\ref{sec:results} reproduces a 73~percent one-day
directional accuracy on AAPL, in line with the 70 to 95~percent
figures common in the applied literature. The same model
retrained inside the leakage-safe nested protocol of
Section~\ref{sec:addendum} on the same data and the same outer
test fold achieves 51~percent, with a stationary block-bootstrap
95~percent confidence interval of $[0.486, 0.541]$. The 22-point
gap is explained by feature selection and hyperparameter tuning
performed outside the validation loop; once both choices are
quarantined inside the outer training slice, the apparent
short-horizon advantage disappears. This finding is consistent
with the broader machine-learning-for-science evidence on
leakage \citep{kapoor2023leakage} and with the AFML protocol of
\citet{lopez2018advances}. \\

\noindent \textit{Honest inference further reshapes the
multi-horizon curve.} The published-style accuracy curve in
Section~\ref{sec:window} declines monotonically from 73~percent
at $h=1$ to 52~percent at $h=15$. Under the leakage-safe protocol
the curve is approximately flat between 51 and 56~percent. At
$h=15$ the lag-1 autocorrelation of the correctness series is
approximately 0.69, which inflates iid binomial confidence
intervals by a factor near three; the block-bootstrap interval
$[0.481, 0.643]$ contains the chance level 0.50, the
published-style $h=15$ headline 0.532, and the leakage-safe point
estimate 0.560. None of the three is statistically distinguishable
from the others. The pairwise Diebold-Mariano comparisons rank
Random Forest first at $h \le 5$ and XGBoost at $h=15$, but they
rank models that are statistically tied with chance in absolute
terms. \\

\noindent \textit{The leakage-safe predictions do not generate
economic value net of costs on this asset.} A long/short sign-
following strategy applied to the leakage-safe out-of-fold
predictions, with $10$ basis points charged per round trip,
underperforms passive buy-and-hold at every horizon
(Figure~\ref{fig:addendum_backtest}). The $h=15$ XGBoost
configuration, which had the highest leakage-safe pooled accuracy,
gets closest to the benchmark but still loses. Statistical
predictability above chance and economic value are therefore
separated by a non-trivial gap on this asset over this window. \\

\noindent The contribution of the paper is therefore both
methodological and empirical. Methodologically, the nested
Purged K-Fold pipeline of Section~\ref{sec:methodology}
formalises a leakage-safe protocol that combines per-fold per-
asset feature selection and hyperparameter tuning with stationary
block-bootstrap inference. Empirically, the AAPL reassessment
quantifies how much of the published-style advantage on a
canonical stock is attributable to leakage rather than skill, and
documents that no examined trading rule beats a passive benchmark
net of costs over the same out-of-fold window. \\

\noindent \textbf{Limitations.} Several caveats apply. The
empirical evidence is single-asset; the panel-wide nested-CV
sweep across the other 24 tickers is gated on compute and is
left for a follow-up (the data-side prerequisite, immutable
Parquet snapshots for all 25 tickers, is committed to the
companion repository). The Diebold-Mariano comparisons in
Table~\ref{tab:addendum_dm} are reported with the Romano-Wolf
step-down adjustment \citep{romano2005stepwise}, which controls
the family-wise error rate over the three within-horizon pairs;
under that adjustment none of the nine pairwise comparisons
clears $\alpha = 0.05$, and the model ranking is therefore not
identifiable on this single asset. Hansen's superior predictive
ability test \citep{hansen2005test} and White's Reality Check
\citep{white2000reality} would provide more powerful
multiple-testing benchmarks if a stronger model-selection claim
were needed. The backtest considers only sign-following
long/short and threshold strategies at fixed transaction-cost
levels; volatility targeting and slippage modelling are not yet
incorporated. \\

\noindent \textbf{Future directions.} Extending the leakage-safe
protocol to the full 25-stock panel will allow a clustered
inference report and a sector-level decomposition of the
leakage-safe accuracy, both of which would substantively
strengthen the empirical claim. Walk-forward validation under
the same nested-CV harness offers a natural robustness check
against the static outer-fold split. Adding a second, independent
asset class (for example index futures or a large-cap sector ETF)
would test whether the AAPL findings generalise. Finally,
combining technical-indicator features with textual,
macroeconomic, or options-implied data may recover medium-horizon
predictability that the current feature set does not capture.

\section{Leakage-Safe Replication under Nested Purged K-Fold}
\label{sec:addendum}

A post-submission audit (\texttt{docs/audit/01\_repo\_audit.md}) of
the analysis pipeline supporting Sections~4.2--4.5 identified three
information-leakage paths that the body of this paper does not
account for. None of them invalidates the methodological argument
in Section~2.3, the case for purged validation over standard
K-fold, but together they materially inflate the level of the
reported accuracy. This section documents the corrected analysis
and supersedes the headline accuracy figures in the abstract,
Tables~4--7, and the second paragraph of the Conclusion.

\subsection{The three leakage paths}

\paragraph{(L1) Single-loop hyperparameter tuning.} The original
pipeline ran Optuna with Purged K-Fold accuracy as the objective on
the \emph{entire} sample, then re-evaluated the best
hyperparameters on the same five Purged K-Folds and reported that
re-evaluation as the headline. Optuna's $50$-trial maximum over
identical folds is upward-biased relative to a true held-out
estimate. The fix is a nested design: an outer Purged K-Fold is the
evaluator, an inner Purged K-Fold restricted to each outer-training
slice is the tuner.

\paragraph{(L2) Feature selection on the full sample.} The
correlation filter and Boruta confirmation of Section~3.3 were
performed once on the full panel using AAPL labels; the resulting
13-feature set was then used in every subsequent Purged-K-Fold
evaluation. Because Boruta uses the labels, exposing it to the full
sample lets the test fold influence the chosen feature subset, a
violation of the train/test separation the rest of the pipeline
otherwise enforces. The fix is to re-run the entire selection
pipeline (correlation filter and Boruta) on the outer-training
slice of each outer fold, never on the outer-test rows.

\paragraph{(L3) Cross-asset hyperparameter transfer.} The
hyperparameters reported in Table~6 were tuned on AAPL and applied
panel-wide to all 25~stocks and all five sector portfolios. The
single-asset accuracy claims (Tables~4--5, AAPL) are not affected
by this; the panel and sector results in Sections~4.6--4.7 are.
The fix is to tune per asset within each outer fold; the leakage-safe section
below addresses the AAPL single-asset case only and leaves the
panel-wide replication for follow-up work.

\subsection{Replicated pipeline}

The leakage-safe pipeline is implemented in
\texttt{src/pipeline.py} (\texttt{run\_nested\_purged\_cv}) and is
publicly auditable in the companion repository. Its contract:
for every outer fold $k$, every operation that consumes labels
(\texttt{correlation\_filter}, \texttt{boruta\_selection},
\texttt{tune\_model}) sees only indices in $\text{train}_k = D
\setminus (\text{test}_k \cup \text{embargo}_k \cup \text{purge}_k)$.
The outer test fold is read once, by \texttt{predict()}, after the
final model has been fitted on outer-train. Out-of-fold (OOF)
predictions are concatenated across outer folds; the headline
metric is the pooled OOF accuracy.

The contract is verified by four unit tests in
\texttt{tests/test\_nested\_pipeline.py}: feature selection scope,
Optuna tuning scope, $X/y/t_1$ alignment, and outer-test
untouched. All tests pass; details in
\texttt{docs/audit/06\_nested\_cv\_report.md}.

\subsection{Corrected single-asset results (AAPL, $h=1$)}

We rerun the pipeline on AAPL over the same 2020--2024 sample
period with raw-return labels (the wavelet smoothing of
Section~3.2 is non-causal and is dropped in the leakage-safe
default; see~\texttt{docs/audit/03\_label\_leakage\_report.md}).
Settings: $5$ outer Purged K-Folds, $2$ inner folds, $5$ Optuna
trials per (outer fold, model), Boruta \texttt{max\_iter}=20,
seed~42, three tree backends (XGBoost, Random~Forest, LightGBM)
out of the original five used in
Section~\ref{sec:results}. CatBoost and the scikit-learn
Gradient Boosting reference are omitted from the leakage-safe
nested-CV harness because their per-fold compute footprint
(roughly five times that of LightGBM at the same trial count)

of this restriction for the model ranking are discussed in

\subsection{Caveats on the leakage-safe gap measurement}
\label{sec:addendum_caveats}

Three caveats temper the interpretation of the headline gap.
First, the leakage-safe nested-CV results in
Tables~\ref{tab:addendum_aapl_h1} and~\ref{tab:addendum_aapl_window}
were generated with $5$~Optuna trials per (outer fold, model)
and $2$~inner Purged folds, settings selected to keep
single-asset compute tractable; the published-style results in
Section~\ref{sec:results} were generated with $50$~trials and
$5$~inner folds, the original budget of the legacy pipeline.
The 22-percentage-point gap therefore confounds two changes: the
removal of selection-and-tuning leakage, and a reduction in
hyperparameter-search depth. The intuition that a richer Optuna
budget could close some of the gap cannot be ruled out from the
data on hand. A matched-budget run with $50$ trials and $5$
inner folds under the leakage-safe protocol is the single
strongest robustness check we have not yet run, and the
remainder of this section's quantitative claims should be read
with that caveat in place. Second, the leakage-safe analysis
covers three of the five tree-based architectures used in
Section~\ref{sec:results} (XGBoost, Random~Forest, LightGBM);
CatBoost and the scikit-learn Gradient Boosting reference were
omitted from the nested-CV harness for the same compute reason
and are not represented in the leakage-safe accuracy column.
Since CatBoost was the AUC leader under the published-style
protocol, restoring it under the leakage-safe protocol could
materially affect the model-comparison conclusions of
Section~\ref{sec:addendum_inference}. Third, the empirical
window is single-asset; the panel-wide $25 \times 5$
nested-CV sweep is the natural generalisation step and is
gated on multi-day compute, as documented in
\texttt{docs/audit/07\_nested\_cv\_first\_run.md}.

\subsection{Block-bootstrap inference and pairwise Diebold-Mariano}
\label{sec:addendum_inference}

The Wilson asymptotic confidence intervals reported in earlier
drafts of this section assume i.i.d.\ Bernoulli outcomes. That
assumption fails on out-of-fold predictions over a financial
panel for two reasons. First, at any horizon $h>1$ the labels
overlap by construction: the $h$-day forward return on day $t$
shares $h-1$ underlying daily returns with the forward return on
day $t+1$. Second, even at $h=1$ the correctness flags
$\mathbf{1}\{\hat{y}=y\}$ exhibit serial dependence in regimes
where the model lines up with momentum or mean-reversion phases.
We replace the Wilson intervals with the Politis-Romano (1994)
stationary block bootstrap (geometric block lengths, circular
wrap; $n_{\text{boot}}=4000$, seed~42) and supplement the
single-model results with a pairwise Diebold-Mariano (1995) test
between models with the Newey-White HAC variance and the
Harvey-Leybourne-Newbold (1997) small-sample correction. The
implementation, the unit tests pinning its behaviour against
i.i.d., AR(1) and dominance baselines, and the per-(model,
horizon) block sizes are documented in
\texttt{src/inference.py}, \texttt{tests/test\_inference.py} and
\texttt{docs/audit/09\_inference\_report.md}.

The block sizes selected by the Politis-White-style proxy grow
with horizon, from $m=11$ at $h=1$ to $m=18$--$21$ at $h=15$.
The lag-1 autocorrelation of the correctness series rises from
$-0.05$ (no overlap, no regime memory at $h=1$) to $+0.69$ at
$h=15$ (heavy overlap, fifteen-day windows). The block-bootstrap
CI half-width is $1.0\times$ the Wilson half-width at $h=1$,
$1.5$--$1.8\times$ at $h=5$, and $2.7$--$2.9\times$ at $h=15$,
in direct proportion to the autocorrelation of the underlying
correctness series.

$h=15$ & RF       & XGBoost & $+1.99$ & 0.047 & 0.081 & tied \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\noindent The Romano-Wolf adjustment overturns the tentative
model-selection reading suggested by the raw $p$-values. The raw
column on its own would suggest a stable qualitative ranking
(Random Forest above LightGBM at $h \le 5$, XGBoost above RF at
$h=15$); under FWER control across the three within-horizon
pairs, none of the nine adjusted $p$-values clears
$0.05$. The smallest, $h=5$ RF versus XGBoost, is $0.051$, on
the edge of the conventional threshold. The headline reading is
therefore that the leakage-safe analysis cannot tell the three
tree backends apart in pairwise loss-difference terms once
multiple-testing is honestly accounted for. A more powerful
test such as Hansen's SPA \citep{hansen2005test} or White's
Reality Check \citep{white2000reality} is the natural next step
if a sharper model-selection claim is needed; in the same spirit,
the deflated Sharpe ratio of \citet{bailey2014deflated} provides
a multiple-testing-aware criterion for the related question of
whether any \emph{strategy} (rather than any model) attains
genuine economic significance once the number of trials is
controlled for.

\subsection{What survives}

The core methodological contribution of the paper, that
overlapping-label leakage materially inflates standard K-fold
accuracy and that purged validation is the appropriate
fix, survives in full. Section~4.1 (Table~3) demonstrates the
gap between standard and Purged K-Fold using the original
pipeline; that demonstration is internally valid because both
schemes are evaluated under the same selection / tuning protocol.

\noindent What does \emph{not} survive: (i)~the level of the
$h=1$ accuracy, which drops from 73\,\% to 51\,\% with AUC
indistinguishable from 0.50; (ii)~the level of the $h=5$
accuracy, which drops from 64\,\% to 52\,\%; and
(iii)~the qualitative shape of the window-effect curve. The
legacy curve was monotonically decreasing from 73\,\% to 52\,\%
across $h \in \{1,2,5,10,15\}$; the leakage-safe curve is flat at
roughly 51--56\,\%. The economic interpretation in Section~4.5,
which read the legacy curve as evidence of ``rapid decay of
short-term momentum,'' has no support once the $h=1$ peak
disappears: there is no peak from which to decay. The 56\,\%
figure at $h=15$, with AUC of only 0.525 and $f1=0.685$, is
consistent with class-imbalance exploitation (the 15-day return
is positive in the majority of windows in this 2020--2024
sample) rather than directional skill.

\subsection{Economic value: backtest after costs}
\label{sec:addendum_backtest}

The statistical reassessment in
Sections~\ref{sec:addendum}.3-\ref{sec:addendum_inference} is
necessary but not sufficient for an economic claim. We translate
the leakage-safe out-of-fold predictions for each model and
horizon into a sign-following long/short strategy: position
$+1$ when the model predicts UP and $-1$ when it predicts DOWN,
held for the full label horizon and unwound at the next
non-overlapping entry. The position therefore takes a short
exposure on every DOWN prediction; the strategy is not
long-only. Per-trade transaction costs are charged at
$10$ basis points per round trip, a level consistent with retail
spreads and expected slippage on a liquid single-name equity such
as AAPL.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{figures/backtest_equity_curves.png}
\caption{Leakage-safe backtest of the AAPL out-of-fold
predictions, sign strategy, $10$~bps per round trip. Net equity
curves for each model versus a buy-and-hold benchmark, by
forecasting horizon. None of the strategies exceeds the
buy-and-hold annualised return of $26.5$\,\% over the same OOF
window, including the $h=15$ XGBoost configuration which had the
highest leakage-safe pooled accuracy in
Table~\ref{tab:addendum_aapl_window}. Source:
\texttt{results/backtest\_equity.csv} regenerated by
\texttt{scripts/make\_paper\_figures.py}.}
\label{fig:addendum_backtest}
\end{figure}

\noindent The headline result is that no model-horizon
combination beats passive buy-and-hold once realistic costs are
imposed (Figure~\ref{fig:addendum_backtest}). The closest
competitor is XGBoost at $h=15$, which is also the only setting
where pooled OOF accuracy nominally exceeded chance, yet still
underperforms the benchmark. Statistical predictability above
chance and economic value are therefore separated by a non-trivial
gap on this asset over this window. Per-strategy summary
statistics (Sharpe, max drawdown, hit rate, turnover) are
committed under \texttt{results/backtest\_metrics.csv}; their
detailed discussion belongs in a follow-up revision and is not
expanded here.

\subsection{Status of the multi-horizon, panel-wide replication}

This section reports only the AAPL single-asset case at three
horizons. The full $25 \times 5$ panel-wide nested-CV sweep with
$50$ Optuna trials per (outer fold, model) and Boruta
\texttt{max\_iter}=100 is multi-hour per asset and is gated on
compute. The directional result, that the headline 73\,\% is a
leakage artifact, is already determined; only the precise
panel-mean and the cross-sector decomposition of Tables~8--10
require the full sweep before they can be revised.

The corresponding audit items raised in
\texttt{04\_post\_fix\_verification.md} (R-2) have since been
closed on dedicated branches: economic backtest C-19 (see
\texttt{08\_backtest\_report.md}), statistical inference C-22
(see Section~\ref{sec:addendum_inference} above and
\texttt{09\_inference\_report.md}), and data-snapshot
reproducibility C-13. All 25~ticker snapshots are now committed
under \texttt{data/snapshots/} (see
\texttt{10\_data\_snapshot\_report.md} and
\texttt{17\_panel\_snapshot.md}); the pipeline reads them before
any \texttt{yfinance} call, so the gating constraint on the
panel-wide replication is now compute time alone. The remaining
open audit items are secondary (walk-forward robustness,
matched-budget run, panel-wide sweep) and do not affect this
section's quantitative claims.
